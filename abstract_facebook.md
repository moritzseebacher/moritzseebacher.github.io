## Abstract

Does access to online social networking platforms affect young workers' careers? Exploiting the staggered introduction of Facebook across U.S. colleges in a generalized difference-in-differences design, I estimate the causal impact of Facebook access at labor market entry on the career trajectories of U.S. business and economics students. Using detailed résumé data from nearly 900,000 public LinkedIn profiles, I find that students with early access to Facebook are more likely to receive early-career promotions, work in higher-paying jobs, and reach senior leadership roles in their mid-30s to early 40s. I provide evidence that the long-run career effects can be explained by (i) a smoother college-to-work transition, with college peers helping each other find high-quality early-career jobs, (ii) accelerated skill accumulation due to early-career sorting into high-training firms, and (iii) improved early-career worker-firm match quality, enabling students to accumulate firm-specific human capital and advance within their organizations. The results highlight how work-related use of online social networks can facilitate entry into the labor market and generate long-run career benefits for young workers.
